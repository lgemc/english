# 6. Mastery Level (C2)

**Chapter summary:** Native-like command of English with precision in all contexts, including academic, professional, and literary language.

## Nuances in Tense and Aspect

**Subtle distinctions:**
- He has gone (still away) vs He has been (returned)
- I'm seeing her tomorrow (arranged) vs I see what you mean (understand)
- I've been reading (duration/incomplete) vs I've read (completion/result)

**Future in the past:**
- would: I thought it would rain
- was going to: She was going to call, but she forgot

**Habitual past:**
- would: When I was young, I would visit my grandparents every summer
- used to: I used to smoke (no longer true)

**Advanced progressive uses:**
- Present simple for dramatic narrative: So I walk into the room and there's this guy...
- Modal perfects: She may have been being followed (passive continuous perfect modal)

## Advanced Stylistic Devices

**Fronting for emphasis:**
- Rare though it is, it does happen
- Gorgeous the sunset was
- Into the room walked a stranger

**Ellipsis:** omitting understood words
- Want some? (Do you want some?)
- Going? (Are you going?)
- Better late than never (It's better to be late than never arrive)

**Substitution:**
- one/ones: I like the red one
- so: I think so, I hope so
- do so: She asked me to leave, so I did so

**Literary present:** discussing literature/film
- In Hamlet, the prince kills Claudius
- The movie ends with them walking into the sunset

## Hedging and Diplomatic Language

**Softening claims:**
- It would seem/appear that...
- There is a tendency to suggest...
- It could be argued that...
- To some extent, arguably, possibly, presumably

**Expressing doubt/caution:**
- I'm not entirely convinced that...
- I have some reservations about...
- One might question whether...

**Disagreeing politely:**
- I see your point, but/however...
- That's one way of looking at it, though...
- I'm afraid I have to disagree (somewhat)
- With respect, I think...

**Downtoners:** rather, somewhat, fairly, quite, a bit, slightly, to a certain extent

## Complex Sentence Structures

**Multiple subordination:**
- The report, which was written by the team that we hired last month, suggests that the project, which had been delayed due to unforeseen circumstances, should now proceed

**Non-finite clauses:**
- Participles: Walking down the street, umbrella in hand, she contemplated her options
- Infinitives: To succeed in this field requires dedication
- Bare infinitives: I saw him leave the building

**Correlative structures:**
- Not only...but also: Not only was he late, but he also forgot the documents
- No sooner...than: No sooner had I arrived than the phone rang
- Scarcely/Hardly...when: Scarcely had we begun when it started raining
- The more...the more: The more you practice, the better you become

## Idiomatic Language and Metaphor

**Advanced idioms:**
- jump on the bandwagon (join a trend)
- sit on the fence (remain neutral)
- throw in the towel (give up)
- the elephant in the room (obvious problem ignored)
- a can of worms (complex problem)
- cut corners (compromise quality)

**Metaphorical language:**
- Dead metaphors: foot of the mountain, head of the company
- Fresh metaphors: Her words were a lighthouse in the storm
- Extended metaphors: Life is a journey; we face crossroads, take detours...

**Similes:** as brave as a lion, like a fish out of water

## Pragmatics and Implicature

**Conversational implicature:** implied meaning
- "Can you pass the salt?" (request, not yes/no question)
- "It's cold in here" (indirect request to close window/turn on heat)

**Presupposition:**
- "Have you stopped smoking?" (presupposes you smoked)
- "The king of France is bald" (presupposes France has a king)

**Politeness strategies:**
- Positive: showing solidarity (We're in this together)
- Negative: respecting autonomy (If you don't mind, could you...)
- Off-record: indirect hints (It's getting late â†’ time to leave)

**Speech acts:**
- Locutionary: literal meaning
- Illocutionary: intended function (promise, threat, request)
- Perlocutionary: actual effect on listener

## Register Variation and Code-Switching

**Frozen:** fixed, unchanging (Lord's Prayer, wedding vows, legal documents)

**Formal:** one-way, official
- Use: Academic papers, legal contracts, ceremonies
- Features: passive voice, long complex sentences, technical vocabulary, no contractions

**Consultative:** professional but interactive
- Use: Business meetings, doctor-patient, teacher-student
- Features: standard grammar, some technical terms, respectful tone

**Casual:** informal, relaxed
- Use: Friends, family, peers
- Features: contractions, slang, colloquialisms, sentence fragments

**Intimate:** private language between close individuals
- Use: Close relationships
- Features: inside jokes, non-verbal cues, shared understanding

**Code-switching:** alternating between registers/languages based on context
- Audience design: adjusting speech for listener
- Topic shifts: formal for business, casual for personal
- Bilingual switches: mixing languages within conversation

**Markers of expertise:** appropriate register choice demonstrates linguistic sophistication and social awareness
